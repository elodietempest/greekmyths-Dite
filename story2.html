    Long before mortals built ships or whispered prayers to the tide, the world was ruled by the Titans. Among them stood Cronus, who feared a prophecy that one of his children would overthrow him. To prevent this fate, he swallowed each child at birth. But his wife, Rhea, refused to lose them all. One by one, her children were hidden or saved. Among those rescued was her fierce and tempest-hearted son, Poseidon. After growing in secret, Poseidon joined his brother Zeus in rebellion. The war between the Olympians and the Titans shook the heavens and earth in a clash known as the Titanomachy. For ten years it raged, thunder splitting mountains and oceans boiling with fury. When the Titans fell, the three brothers drew lots to divide the cosmos. Zeus claimed the sky. Hades took the underworld. And Poseidon—powerful, proud, and restless—claimed the vast and endless sea. With his mighty trident, forged by the Cyclopes, Poseidon could shatter cliffs, summon storms, and cause the earth itself to quake.

    Thus he became known not only as Lord of the Sea—but also as the Earth-Shaker. Though ruler of the oceans, Poseidon’s heart was as unpredictable as the waves. Sailors both worshiped and feared him. They offered bulls and poured wine into the sea, praying for calm waters. For when angered, Poseidon unleashed devastation. He built his palace beneath the waves near the island of Euboea, a shimmering hall of coral and gold. There he lived with his queen, Amphitrite, surrounded by sea nymphs and creatures of the deep. But Poseidon’s pride was legendary. When the city of Athens sought a divine patron, Poseidon struck the Acropolis with his trident, producing either a saltwater spring—or in some tales, the first horse, a creature of strength and war. Yet Athena offered the olive tree, symbol of peace and prosperity. The people chose her gift. Enraged by rejection, Poseidon flooded the plains in bitterness. His wrath was not quick to fade. Of all mortals who felt his fury, none suffered more than Odysseus, king of Ithaca. During his long journey home from the Trojan War, Odysseus encountered the Cyclops Polyphemus—a monstrous giant who happened to be Poseidon’s son. To escape being eaten, Odysseus blinded the creature and fled.

    When Poseidon learned of this insult, his fury churned like a rising storm. From his palace beneath the sea, he swore that Odysseus would not reach home easily. And so began the torment. Of all mortals who felt his fury, none suffered more than Odysseus, king of Ithaca. Winds howled. Ships shattered. Waves rose like mountains. For ten long years, Odysseus wandered the seas, tossed from island to island. Each time he glimpsed hope, Poseidon would stir the waters once more. Even when Odysseus finally neared home, Poseidon summoned a final storm, smashing his raft and casting him into the merciless deep. Only the mercy of other gods spared the hero from drowning. Such was the price of defying the Lord of the Sea. Despite his wrath, Poseidon was not purely cruel. He gave humanity horses, taught them mastery over waves, and granted safe passage to those who honored him properly. Yet the sea has never forgotten his temper. When earthquakes tremble beneath coastal lands, the ancients said it was Poseidon striking the earth with his trident. When storms roll suddenly across calm waters, they believed his anger had been stirred once more. To this day, the ocean remains both beautiful and terrifying calm as
