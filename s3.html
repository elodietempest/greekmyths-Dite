    Before the age of mortals, when the world was ruled by Titans, there lived a being unlike the rest—Prometheus, whose name meant Forethought. He was the son of Iapetus and brother to Atlas and Epimetheus. When war erupted between the Titans and the Olympian gods, Prometheus did something unexpected—he sided with Zeus. He foresaw the fall of the Titans and believed the new ruler of the cosmos would govern with wisdom and justice. After the Olympians triumphed, the earth lay quiet and empty of intelligent life. Prometheus looked upon the rivers, forests, and mountains and felt that something was missing. From clay and water, he shaped figures in the image of the gods. Carefully he molded their hands, their faces, their fragile bodies. Then he breathed life into them. Thus, humanity was born. But humans were weak. They had no claws to defend themselves, no fur to protect them from winter, no wings to escape danger. Epimetheus had already given the best traits to animals. Humanity was left with nothing but bare skin and curious minds. Prometheus saw their suffering

    Page 2

    and he resolved to help them. Though Zeus ruled from Mount Olympus, Prometheus grew uneasy with his growing pride. To test the king of gods, Prometheus devised a trick during a great sacrifice at Mecone. He slaughtered an ox and divided it into two portions. In one pile, he placed the rich meat but concealed it beneath the animal’s unappealing stomach lining. In the other, he piled the bare bones but covered them in shining white fat so they looked tempting. He then invited Zeus to choose which portion would belong to the gods in future sacrifices. Zeus, seeing the beautiful layer of fat, chose the bones. When he realized the deception, his anger burned fiercely. From that day forward, humans kept the nourishing meat for themselves while offering bones and fat to the gods. Enraged by being tricked, Zeus decided to punish mankind. He withheld fire from the earth, declaring that mortals were unworthy of divine flame. Without fire, humans trembled in darkness. They could not cook food, forge tools, or warm themselves against the cold. Civilization halted before it could begin. Prometheus watched them suffer. And he could not bear it. Determined to defy Zeus, Prometheus climbed secretly to Olympus. Some say he stole a spark from the blazing forge of Hephaestus.

    Others say he lit a fennel stalk from the chariot of the sun. Hidden within the hollow reed, the flame glowed unseen. He carried it down to earth—and gave it to humanity. For the first time, night was no longer absolute. Flames danced in the darkness. Food was cooked. Metals were shaped. Art and craft were born. The spark of civilization ignited. Zeus soon discovered the theft. His fury shook the heavens. If Prometheus had empowered humanity, Zeus would ensure that power came with suffering. He ordered Hephaestus to craft a woman of breathtaking beauty from clay. The gods bestowed gifts upon her—grace, charm, curiosity, persuasion. She was named Pandora. Zeus sent Pandora to Epimetheus, who—despite Prometheus’ warning—accepted her. She carried with her a sealed jar. Overcome by curiosity, she opened it, releasing sickness, sorrow, greed, jealousy, and toil into the world. Only Hope remained inside. Thus, humanity received both fire and suffering. For Prometheus himself, Zeus devised a far crueler punishment. He ordered him chained to a lonely cliff in the Caucasus Mountains. Unbreakable bonds held the Titan against the cold stone. Each day, a massive eagle descended from the sky.

    Page 4

    It tore into Prometheus’ side and devoured his liver. Because he was immortal, the organ regenerated every night—only to be eaten again the next day. Agony without end. Yet Prometheus never cried out for mercy. He never regretted his gift. For far below, he could see smoke rising from human hearths—proof that his suffering had meaning. Ages passed. At last, the hero Heracles journeyed through the mountains during his labors. Seeing the Titan’s torment, he shot the eagle with a single arrow and shattered the chains. Zeus allowed the release—but demanded a symbol of submission. Prometheus was forced to wear a ring made from his broken chains, set with a fragment of the rock, so that he would remain eternally bound in memory. And so Prometheus became more than a Titan. He became a symbol of rebellion against tyranny, of sacrifice for knowledge, and of the eternal flame of progress. For every spark of fire, every forge, every candle in the dark— carries the legacy of the Titan who dared to defy a god.
